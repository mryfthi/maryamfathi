About

Turning tumor complexity into readable signals

Highly motivated and detail-oriented, with a strong molecular and cellular biology foundation and a growing computational toolkit. I bridge wet-lab and dry-lab to build the methods that make cancer legible.

What I do

In the Leshchiner lab at Boston University, my research focuses on developing and applying advanced genomic, computational, and AI approaches to study tumor evolution using patient samples. I'm building a long-read single-cell DNA and methylation sequencing method to detect rare somatic mutations, resolve cell-specific epigenetic states, and reconstruct clonal relationships within tumors.

By integrating genomic, epigenomic, and other multi-omics data, I investigate tumor heterogeneity, subclonal dynamics, and the molecular events behind cancer progression and metastasis — with the goal of identifying clinically relevant biomarkers and therapeutic vulnerabilities.

Science in motion

The tools I build, animated

A playful look at the biology behind my work — reading single-cell genomes, editing one base at a time, and watching a mutation ripple through a tumor.

🧫 Single-cell DNA sequencing & methylation

One cell is isolated in focus, then its genome is read base-by-base while methylation marks are mapped in parallel — resolving clonal states and epigenetic identity together.

✂️ CRISPR base editing

A cute Cas9 protein, guided by its gRNA, docks on the target and rewrites a single base — A → G — a precise edit for modelling and correcting mutations.

🌱 Somatic mutations & clonal expansion

A mutation arises in a single cell — its DNA lights up in the lens — and that clone expands through the tumour, one division at a time.
